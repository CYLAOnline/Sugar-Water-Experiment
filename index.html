The Fundamental Process of Science

The Scientific Method

Have you ever wondered how scientists are able to develop all of the things they do?
The process is quite simple and it is called the scientific method. The scientific method
consists of six steps:

(1) Asking a question based on observation.
(2) Formulating a hypothesis or educated guess.
(3) Conducting an experiment. Note: An experiment has three types of variables.
  • Controlled Variable -- The variables in an experiment that are held constant
  • Independent Variable -- The variable that is intentionally manipulated
  • Dependent Variable -- The variable that is held as a function of the independent variable
(4) Analyzing data
(5) Formulating a conclusion
(6) Communicating results to an audience

Let's dive deep into the scientific method through exemplifying with the classic sugar in water example.


What's the Scenario?

It's a hot summer day and you decide to go play tennis with one of your tennis buddies.
You dissolve some sugar in some water that you got from the fridge, which is very cold. It takes
a decent amount of time to dissolve. You then decide to use water from the faucet which is room temperature
and this time it does not take as long to dissolve. Curious as to why this occured, you text your tennis buddy
and cancel the appointment as your scientific mind has just been activated.

Step One: Asking a Question Based on Observation

Probed by this seemingly strange occurance, you ask yourself:

Why did the sugar dissolve faster in the water that was of high temperature?
Could it be that sugar dissolves faster as temperature increases?

Step Two: Formulating a Hypothesis

As the old saying goes, fortune favors the bold. You decide that the temperature increase
of the water lead to the increase in dissolvation rate of the sugar. You form the following
hypothesis in the form of an IF/THEN statement:

If I increase the temperature of the water, the sugar will dissolve faster

Step Three: Conducting an Experiment

Now that the hypothesis has been formed, you decide to set up an experiment in congruence with
with the guidelines of the scientific method.

Controlled Variables Indepdent Variable Dependent Variable
  • Sugar Brand
  • Cup Type
  • Water Volume
 Water Temperature Sugar Dissolvation Rate
Simply put, you have three cups of 1L of water, one at 0 degrees, 37 degrees, and 100 degrees. You dissolve
the same volume of sugar in each cup and time the time it takes to dissolve.

Step Four: Analyzing Data

The results are tabulated as follows:

Cup # Temperature (Celsius) Volume of Water (L) Dissolvation Time (s)
One 0 1 45
Two 37 1 18
Three 100 1 3

The temperature of 0 degrees yielded a dissolvation rate of 45 seconds
The temperature of 37 degrees yielded a dissolvation rate of 18 seconds
The temperature of 100 degrees yielded a dissolvation rate of 3 seconds

Step Five: Forming a Conclusion

Based on the data, you conclude that your hypothesis was correct.
As temperature increases, the dissolvation rate of sugar increases as well.

Step Six: Communicating Your Results

After 45 minutes your experiment is complete. You run off to the tennis court and tell your tennis buddy
what you have just learned.
